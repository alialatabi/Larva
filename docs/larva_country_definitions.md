## Design Approach

The 18 countries are organized into three tiers of economic development. This tiering is a design tool — players never see tier labels. They see 18 countries with different profiles and pick the one that fits their strategy.

**Tier 1 — Developed (5 countries):** High infrastructure, high spending power, expensive labor and real estate, higher taxes. Destination markets — where goods get consumed, where premium services thrive, where Finance and Technology dominate. Manufacturing here is expensive. Players come here to sell, not to produce.

**Tier 2 — Emerging (8 countries):** Medium across most modifiers with diverse identities. Some lean toward manufacturing, some toward trade, some toward services. Most players will start and stay in Tier 2 countries. Flexible enough for multiple strategies, competitive enough to be interesting.

**Tier 3 — Developing (5 countries):** Low infrastructure, low spending power, cheap labor and land, low taxes. Production economies — where raw materials are abundant and manufacturing margins are fattest. The catch: getting goods OUT is expensive and slow. Players who solve the logistics problem here build empires. Players who don't bleed money into transport costs.

## Modifier Reference

Each country is defined by seven modifiers established in the Country System framework:

| Modifier | Description | Range |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Tax Rate | Base rate for progressive bracket system (applied to business net profits per tick) | 5% – 25% |
| Labor Cost Modifier | Multiplier on all base salaries | 0.7x – 1.4x |
| Real Estate Cost Modifier | Multiplier on base property costs (rent and purchase) | 0.6x – 1.5x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Determines how much local players are willing to pay; affects which quality tiers are viable | Low / Medium / High |
| Resource Availability | 1–3 natural resource categories locally abundant at base price | Per country |
| Infrastructure Rating | Affects local logistics speed and cost; affects international shipment clearance speed | Low / Medium / High |
| Industry Specialization | 1–2 sectors receive an output efficiency bonus in that country | Per country (10–20%) |

### 1. VALEN

**Identity:** The financial capital. Money flows through Valen — banks, investment firms, insurance companies, and legal practices dominate. The most expensive place to operate anything, but the deepest pockets in the game. You don't manufacture here. You manage wealth here.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 22% |
| Labor Cost | 1.35x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.5x |
| Consumer Spending Power | High |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Chemical Compounds |
| Specialization | Finance (15%), Professional Services (15%) |

**Tier:** Developed

**Strategic Profile:** Players running Private Banks, Investment Firms, Accounting Firms, and Legal Firms thrive here. The high spending power means premium retail can work, but the labor and rent costs crush any manufacturing attempt. The single resource (Chemical Compounds) exists mainly as an export — pharmaceutical manufacturers in other countries import from Valen's Central Corp operations. The real product Valen exports is financial services: loans, investment management, and insurance policies sold to businesses in other countries.

**Who starts here:** Players who want to build a financial empire. Also attracts premium retail and restaurant operators willing to pay high costs for high-spending customers.

**Key tradeoff:** Highest potential revenue per customer, but highest costs per tick. A bad week in Valen is more expensive than a bad week anywhere else.

### 2. KORR

**Identity:** The industrial engine. Cheap labor, strong infrastructure, abundant metals and oil. This is where electronics and heavy goods get manufactured. Korr's factories feed the entire game world.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 12% |
| Labor Cost | 0.75x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.85x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Metals & Minerals, Petroleum & Synthetics |
| Specialization | Manufacturing (20%), Logistics & Transport (10%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** The premier Electronics Manufacturing location. Both key raw inputs (metals and petroleum) are locally abundant — no import markup on primary materials. High infrastructure means fast internal logistics and reasonable international shipping times. The Logistics specialization makes freight companies here more efficient, supporting the heavy export volume. Medium spending power means local retail is viable but not exciting — the real money is in B2B contracts shipping electronics to retailers worldwide.

**Who starts here:** Players who want to build manufacturing empires, especially electronics. Also strong for logistics companies servicing the export flow.

**Key tradeoff:** Fantastic for production, mediocre as a consumer market. A Korr manufacturer who also wants retail revenue needs branches in higher-spending countries.

### 3. MERIS

**Identity:** The breadbasket. Cheap ingredients, abundant agriculture, the food capital of the game world. Every restaurant and supermarket chain eventually sources from Meris.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 10% |
| Labor Cost | 0.9x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.8x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Agricultural Products, Natural Fibers |
| Specialization | Food & Hospitality (20%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** Food Manufacturing companies get a 20% output bonus plus locally cheap agricultural inputs — the combination makes Meris the lowest-cost food production hub in the game. Restaurants and cafés also benefit from the specialization bonus. Natural Fibers attract some textile manufacturing, but without Petroleum & Synthetics locally, synthetic blends require imports. The medium infrastructure is the main limitation — international food exports face spoilage risk during extended transit.

**Who starts here:** New players who want to enter the food industry. Food Manufacturing owners who want maximum margin. Restaurant operators who want cheap ingredients and a specialization bonus.

**Key tradeoff:** Dominant in food, limited in everything else. The medium infrastructure means perishable exports to distant countries arrive degraded. Smart Meris food exporters target nearby high-infrastructure countries first.

### 4. THORN

**Identity:** The technology hub. Software firms and data analytics companies cluster here because the specialization bonus makes their products measurably better than competitors elsewhere.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 20% |
| Labor Cost | 1.3x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.3x |
| Consumer Spending Power | High |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Chemical Compounds, Metals & Minerals |
| Specialization | Technology (20%), Education & Training (10%) |

**Tier:** Developed

**Strategic Profile:** Software Firms and Data Analytics Firms get a 20% efficiency bonus — their products are better here, full stop. IT Services companies also benefit. The Education specialization creates a secondary advantage: skill training centers are more effective, which means Thorn produces higher-skilled players faster than most countries. High infrastructure supports the tech sector's zero-logistics advantage (software doesn't ship). The two resources (chemicals, metals) make Thorn a viable secondary manufacturing location, though labor costs make it expensive compared to dedicated manufacturing countries.

**Who starts here:** Tech entrepreneurs. Software developers who want the best product quality. Players pursuing advanced skill tracks through education.

**Key tradeoff:** Technology products built here are superior, but the operating costs are steep. A software firm in Thorn must charge premium subscription fees to justify the overhead. A competitor in a cheaper country produces an inferior product but at half the cost — players must decide which wins.

### 5. CASSEN

**Identity:** The trading post. Sits at the crossroads of everything. The highest logistics efficiency in the game. Goods pass through Cassen faster and cheaper than anywhere else.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 14% |
| Labor Cost | 1.0x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.0x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Timber & Wood |
| Specialization | Logistics & Transport (20%), Retail (10%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** Delivery companies and freight operators get a 20% efficiency bonus — faster deliveries, lower costs, better handling. This makes Cassen the natural transit hub for international trade. The Retail specialization rewards businesses that import manufactured goods from production countries and sell them locally or re-export. Timber makes Cassen a secondary source for construction materials. The perfectly average labor and real estate costs make it viable for almost any business type, but nothing besides logistics gets a structural advantage.

**Who starts here:** Logistics entrepreneurs who want to build shipping empires. Retail operators who want to be the distribution center between manufacturing and consumer countries. Players who value flexibility over specialization.

**Key tradeoff:** Cassen is good at everything, exceptional at logistics. The danger is being average — a Cassen restaurant competes on equal terms with a Meris restaurant that gets a 20% food bonus. Cassen rewards players who build logistics networks and trade infrastructure, not players who want to dominate a single industry.

### 6. DRAVEL

**Identity:** The tax haven. Lowest taxes, cheap operations, but a thin local market and middling infrastructure. Players come here to keep more of what they earn — if they can generate revenue despite the weak local demand.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 5% |
| Labor Cost | 0.85x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.7x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Petroleum & Synthetics |
| Specialization | Finance (15%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** The 5% base tax rate means even the highest bracket players pay dramatically less than in high-tax countries. With the progressive system, a Tycoon-bracket player in Dravel pays an effective 12.5% — versus 40% (capped) in Aurel. Finance specialization attracts investment firms and banks that want maximum retained profit. Petroleum makes Dravel relevant for manufacturers who need synthetics, but the low spending power means local consumers can barely afford Standard tier goods. The real play: headquarters your financial operations here, branch into richer markets for revenue.

**Who starts here:** Tax-optimizing strategists. Finance players who want maximum margin. Manufacturers who need cheap petroleum access and don't care about local sales.

**Key tradeoff:** You keep more money, but there's less money to keep. Low spending power means thin local demand. The player who builds a bank in Dravel and sells financial services to businesses in Valen captures both advantages — low cost base, high revenue market. That's the intended play, but it requires enough capital and skill to operate cross-country.

### 7. LORREN

**Identity:** Build city. Abundant building materials, construction specialization, and a booming real estate market. Every large construction project in the game has some connection to Lorren.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 13% |
| Labor Cost | 0.8x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.9x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Stone & Aggregate, Timber & Wood |
| Specialization | Construction & Real Estate (20%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** Construction Companies get a 20% output bonus — projects complete faster and cheaper. Both key construction raw materials (stone and timber) are locally abundant, eliminating import costs on the two biggest input categories. Cheap labor makes crews affordable. The combination creates the lowest-cost construction environment in the game. Real Estate Agencies and Property Management companies also benefit from the specialization. Medium infrastructure is the constraint for exporting construction services to other countries, but most construction is local by nature — you build where the project is.

**Who starts here:** Construction empire builders. Real estate developers. Players who want to control the physical infrastructure of a country's economy.

**Key tradeoff:** Construction demand is cyclical — it booms when the economy expands and crashes when growth stalls. Lorren's specialization amplifies both directions. A construction company here during a boom is printing money. During a slowdown, the cheap operating costs are the only thing keeping the doors open.

### 8. VESK

**Identity:** The textile floor. Cheapest labor in the game, abundant natural fibers, but terrible infrastructure. Maximum production margins if you can solve the shipping problem.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 7% |
| Labor Cost | 0.7x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.6x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Low |
| Resources | Natural Fibers, Agricultural Products |
| Specialization | Manufacturing (15%) |

**Tier:** Developing

**Strategic Profile:** Textile manufacturing paradise. Lowest labor cost in the game (0.7x) plus locally abundant Natural Fibers means cotton garments produced here have the fattest margins anywhere. Agricultural Products support a secondary food manufacturing operation. The Manufacturing specialization applies to all three manufacturer types, making Vesk viable for any production. The devastating weakness: Low infrastructure means international shipping takes extra ticks and costs more. Perishable goods (food) are especially risky to export. Low spending power means the local market can't absorb Premium goods — everything premium must be exported.

**Who starts here:** Cost-minimizing manufacturers, especially textile. Players willing to accept infrastructure pain for maximum production margins. High-risk, high-reward strategists.

**Key tradeoff:** Cheapest production in the game, most expensive distribution. A textile manufacturer in Vesk producing Premium clothing at rock-bottom cost still needs to ship it to Aurel or Valen where people can afford it — and the low infrastructure means that shipment takes longer and costs more. The margin advantage can be entirely consumed by logistics costs if the player doesn't plan carefully.

### 9. AUREL

**Identity:** The luxury market. Highest spending power, highest taxes, highest labor costs. The place where Premium and Luxury tier goods find their buyers. If you sell the best, you sell it here.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 25% |
| Labor Cost | 1.4x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.5x |
| Consumer Spending Power | High |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Stone & Aggregate |
| Specialization | Retail (20%), Education & Training (10%) |

**Tier:** Developed

**Strategic Profile:** The only country where Luxury tier clothing (the 4th tier exclusive to Clothing Stores) sells at full price consistently. High spending power means customers pay premium prices across every category. The Retail specialization gives all retail types a 20% efficiency bonus — more customers served, better margins. Education specialization makes training centers more effective. Stone & Aggregate is a minor resource export. The crushing costs (1.4x labor, 1.5x real estate, 25% base tax) mean only premium-priced businesses survive. A Basic-tier restaurant in Aurel loses money. A Premium restaurant prints it.

**Who starts here:** Premium and luxury operators. High-end retail chains. Players confident they can charge top prices and maintain quality to justify them.

**Key tradeoff:** Aurel amplifies everything. Great businesses become wildly profitable. Mediocre businesses bleed out fast. The 25% base tax rate means Tycoon-bracket players hit the 40% cap — nearly half their profits go to taxes. The trade: access to the richest customers in the game, but the government takes its cut. Players who can't maintain Premium quality get destroyed by costs that don't scale down.

### 10. NOVA

**Identity:** The balanced center. No extreme in any direction. Moderate costs, moderate demand, moderate infrastructure. The most versatile country in the game — and possibly the most competitive.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 13% |
| Labor Cost | 1.0x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.0x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Agricultural Products, Timber & Wood |
| Specialization | Food & Hospitality (10%), Education & Training (15%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** Nova is the country where every strategy is viable but none is dominant. The 1.0x modifiers on labor and real estate mean businesses operate at baseline economics — no penalty, no bonus. The Education specialization makes it a strong training hub, and Food & Hospitality gets a modest bonus from cheap agricultural inputs. Two resources (agricultural, timber) support food and construction supply chains. The risk: because everything is average, a specialist country always beats Nova at that specialty. A Nova manufacturer competes against Korr's 20% bonus and cheaper labor. A Nova bank competes against Valen's Finance specialization.

**Who starts here:** New players who aren't sure what strategy to pursue. Generalists. Players who want to build a diversified portfolio of businesses. Education entrepreneurs.

**Key tradeoff:** Maximum flexibility, zero structural advantage. Nova players succeed through execution and diversification, not through cost advantages or specialization bonuses. The player who runs three different business types in Nova can pivot when markets shift — something a hyper-specialized Korr manufacturer can't do.

### 11. KETHOS

**Identity:** The mining hub. Rich in metals and stone but underdeveloped. Raw materials flow out cheap, but everything else costs time because the infrastructure can't keep up.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 8% |
| Labor Cost | 0.75x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.7x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Low |
| Resources | Metals & Minerals, Stone & Aggregate |
| Specialization | Manufacturing (15%) |

**Tier:** Developing

**Strategic Profile:** Electronics manufacturers and construction companies love Kethos's resource prices — metals and stone at base cost with no import markup. Cheap labor and real estate make it an affordable production base. The Manufacturing specialization applies broadly. The fatal constraint: Low infrastructure makes every shipment slow and expensive. Electronics manufactured here need to reach Electronics Stores in other countries, and every extra tick in transit is a tick closer to a Technology Generation shift that could devalue the inventory mid-shipment. Construction materials are heavy and expensive to freight internationally.

**Who starts here:** Budget manufacturers willing to accept logistics pain. Construction material suppliers serving nearby medium-infrastructure countries. Players who see infrastructure as a problem to solve, not a reason to avoid.

**Key tradeoff:** Rock-bottom production costs, worst-in-class shipping. A Kethos electronics manufacturer producing Premium equipment at minimal cost can undercut every competitor — IF the goods arrive before the next Technology Generation shift. If they don't, the manufacturer is sitting on obsolete inventory produced at a discount that no longer matters. The country rewards patience, planning, and strong logistics partnerships.

**Identity:** Pharma valley. Chemical compounds and petroleum synthetics — both critical pharmaceutical inputs — are locally abundant. The pharmaceutical manufacturing capital of the game.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|

| Labor Cost | 1.1x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.0x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Chemical Compounds, Petroleum & Synthetics |
| Specialization | Manufacturing (15%), Professional Services (10%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** The only country with both Chemical Compounds and Petroleum & Synthetics locally abundant. The Professional Services bonus is a secondary perk, not a primary draw. Players who aren't in pharma or professional services find Solven unremarkable.

### 13. RYNNA

**Identity:** The emerging port city. Petroleum resources, agricultural products, and a logistics specialization. A developing trade hub with lower costs than established logistics centers like Cassen.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 11% |
| Labor Cost | 0.9x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.85x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Medium |
| Infrastructure | Medium |
| Resources | Petroleum & Synthetics, Agricultural Products |
| Specialization | Logistics & Transport (15%) |

**Tier:** Emerging

**Strategic Profile:** Rynna is the budget Cassen. Logistics companies get a specialization bonus, but less than Cassen's 20%. The advantage: everything costs less. The medium infrastructure is the ceiling — Rynna can handle regional trade efficiently but lacks the high-infrastructure advantage for long-distance international shipping. **Who starts here:** Budget logistics entrepreneurs. Players who want a trade hub without Cassen's costs. Manufacturers who need petroleum and don't want to pay Dravel's thin-market penalty.

**Key tradeoff:** Cheaper than Cassen but slower. A delivery company in Rynna competes with Cassen on price but not on speed. International shipments through Rynna take longer due to medium infrastructure. Players who prioritize cost choose Rynna; players who prioritize speed choose Cassen. This creates a natural market segmentation.

### 15. ZARA

**Identity:** The frontier farm. Cheapest entry point for new players. Agricultural resources, natural fibers, food specialization. Simple, forgiving, and accessible.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 6% |
| Labor Cost | 0.7x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.6x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Low |
| Resources | Agricultural Products, Natural Fibers |
| Specialization | Food & Hospitality (15%) |

**Tier:** Developing

**Strategic Profile:** Zara is designed as the gentlest starting environment. The lowest costs in the game (tied with Vesk on labor, cheapest real estate) combined with a Food specialization and abundant agricultural products means a new player can open a café or small restaurant at minimal risk. The catch: Low spending power means customers can barely afford Standard tier, and Low infrastructure makes export-oriented strategies painful. Natural Fibers attract some textile manufacturing, but the infrastructure constraint limits outbound distribution.

**Who starts here:** Brand new players learning the game. Cost-conscious food industry operators. Players who want maximum runway to experiment before costs overwhelm them.

Players who stay in Zara long-term face a ceiling: the local market can't absorb premium products, and exporting is expensive. The intended arc: start in Zara, learn the systems, then either expand to richer markets or relocate once you have capital and skills.

### 16. ORVAL

**Identity:** The quarry state. Metals and stone in abundance. Construction materials flow cheap, making it a natural partner to construction-heavy economies. But underdeveloped and remote.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 9% |
| Labor Cost | 0.8x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.75x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Low |
| Resources | Metals & Minerals, Stone & Aggregate |
| Specialization | Construction & Real Estate (15%) |

### 17. PELLA

**Identity:** The suburban sprawl. Mid-to-high development, retail-focused, tech-enabled. A consumer economy where people buy things and technology makes businesses run better.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 16% |
| Labor Cost | 1.05x |
| Real Estate Cost | 1.1x |
| Consumer Spending Power | High |
| Infrastructure | High |
| Resources | Metals & Minerals |
| Specialization | Retail (15%), Technology (15%) |

**Tier:** Developed

**Strategic Profile:** Pella combines high spending power with both Retail and Technology specializations. Retail businesses serve affluent consumers more efficiently. Technology companies produce better products for the local business ecosystem. Metals & Minerals attract some electronics manufacturing, but labor costs are above average — Pella is a better place to USE technology than to MANUFACTURE electronics. The 16% tax and 1.05x labor aren't crushing, but they're above median. Players here need to leverage the dual specialization — retail without tech, or tech without retail customers, wastes half the country's advantage.

### 18. TYRN

**Identity:** The raw frontier. Three resources, lowest taxes, cheapest everything — and the worst infrastructure in the game. The ultimate high-risk, high-reward starting position.

| Modifier | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tax Rate | 5% |
| Labor Cost | 0.75x |
| Real Estate Cost | 0.6x |
| Consumer Spending Power | Low |
| Infrastructure | Low |
| Resources | Natural Fibers, Timber & Wood, Stone & Aggregate |
| Specialization | Construction & Real Estate (10%) |

**Tier:** Developing

**Strategic Profile:** Tyrn is the only country with three abundant resources, but all three are in the construction and textile input categories — no metals, no chemicals, no petroleum, no agricultural products. Food must be produced from imported ingredients or sourced from Central Corp at import prices. Electronics equipment comes at import markup. The 5% tax and rock-bottom costs create massive theoretical margins, but the Low infrastructure means getting anything in or out is painfully slow and expensive. The Construction specialization is modest (10%), but combined with two of three construction raw materials being locally abundant, Tyrn is a viable construction base.

**Who starts here:** Risk-tolerant frontier players who see a country with no competition and want to build everything from scratch. Textile manufacturers who need natural fibers. Construction entrepreneurs who want cheap stone and timber.

**Key tradeoff:** Tyrn is the cheapest country to operate in and the hardest to succeed in. Three resources sound generous until you realize none of them are food — every restaurant and café in Tyrn pays import prices on ingredients. The infrastructure penalty applies to everything. Players who build a self-sustaining local economy in Tyrn — food production, retail, logistics, construction — create something almost impossible to compete with because no one else wants to deal with the infrastructure pain. That's the endgame play: be the player who tamed the frontier.

## Emergent Trade Routes

These are not explicitly designed — they fall out of the resource and specialization distribution naturally:

**The Electronics Pipeline:** Korr (manufacture) → Cassen (transit hub) → Aurel/Valen/Pella (sell to premium consumers)

**The Textile Pipeline:** Vesk or Zara (manufacture from local fibers, import petroleum from Rynna/Dravel) → Cassen or Rynna (transit) → Aurel (luxury clothing market)

**The Pharma Pipeline:** Solven (manufacture) → Cassen (transit) → everywhere (every country needs a Pharmacy)

**The Construction Materials Pipeline:** Lorren/Orval/Tyrn (source stone + timber) → freight to wherever construction projects are underway

**The Knowledge Pipeline:** Bren/Thorn (train players and NPCs) → players relocate to production or consumer countries with higher skills

**The Finance Pipeline:** Valen/Dravel (financial services HQ) → branches in every country providing loans, insurance, and investment management

**The Food Pipeline:** Meris/Zara (food manufacturing from local agriculture) → Cassen (transit) → Valen/Aurel/Pella/Thorn/Bren (high-spending markets that import food)

## Design Notes

**No single "best" country:** Every country that excels in one dimension pays for it in another. Aurel has the richest customers but the highest costs. Vesk has the cheapest production but the worst logistics. Cassen has the best shipping but no production bonus. This is by design.

**Natural player distribution:** Economic pressure distributes players. Too many players in any country saturates labor and business markets, compressing margins and pushing players toward underserved countries. Different player strategies favor different countries — a manufacturer and a banker would never rationally choose the same starting country.

**Infrastructure as the great equalizer:** Developing countries have the cheapest costs but the worst infrastructure. This single constraint prevents cheap-country dominance. A player who solves the infrastructure problem (through logistics partnerships, strategic inventory management, or branching into transit hub countries) unlocks enormous value. A player who ignores it bleeds margin into shipping costs.

**Progressive tax interaction:** The base tax rates listed here feed into the progressive bracket system defined in `larva_economy_balance.md`. A 5% base rate (Dravel, Tyrn) creates effective rates from 0% to 12.5%. A 25% base rate (Aurel) creates effective rates from 0% to 40% (capped). The spread between low-tax and high-tax countries is dramatic for wealthy players, creating meaningful relocation incentives at the top of the wealth distribution.
